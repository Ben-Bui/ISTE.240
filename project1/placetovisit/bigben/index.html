History of The House of Parliament and Big Ben

The construction of the Palace of Westminster, home to the British Parliament, began in the mid-19th century after a fire destroyed the previous building. The design by architect Sir Charles Barry and the iconic clock tower, housing the "Great Bell" (commonly known as Big Ben), by Augustus Pugin, became enduring symbols of British democracy.
The Palace of Westminster was constructed in the Gothic Revival style, characterized by its ornate , pointed arches, and spires. The design was a hat off to the rich architectural history of the British Isles.
Contrary to popular belief, "Big Ben" refers to the Great Bell inside the clock tower, not the tower itself. The bell was cast in 1856 and weighs over 13 tons. It is renowned for its resonant chimes.
The Palace of Westminster and the clock tower were officially opened in 1859. Since then, they have stood as iconic symbols of British democracy and government.
In 1987, the Palace of Westminster, including the clock tower housing Big Ben, was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, recognizing its architectural and historical significance.

Why Visit The House of Parliament and Big Ben?

The Palace of Westminster is the center of British democracy and politics. It has served as the meeting place for the UK Parliament for centuries, and its history is intertwined with the nation's democratic journey.
The Great Bell, also known as Big Ben, is a global icon. Its chimes and the towering clock face are the logo of London . Witnessing Big Ben in person is an experience like no other and hearing it chimes.
The Palace is where historic debates, decisions, and momentous events in British history have taken place. It's the heart of the UK's political discourse and the birthplace of important laws.
The Palace of Westminster sits majestically along the banks of the River Thames, offering beautiful views. You can enjoy a stroll along the river and take in the stunning scenery.
The Central Lobby in the Palace is where MPs and visitors often gather. Its intricate mosaic floor, statues, and grandeur make it a striking space to explore. Westminster Bridge: Adjacent to the Palace, Westminster Bridge offers some of the best photo opportunities to capture the Palace and Big Ben in all their glory.
